01 — Central Thesis

The Substitution India Is Not Discussing

This is not a commodity price forecast. It is a strategic dependency analysis asking a systems-level question: what second-order industrial dependencies emerge when a civilisation electrifies? The answer reveals a structural risk that India's current policy architecture is not designed to detect.

"Critical mineral dependency may become more strategically rigid and technologically irreversible than oil dependency — despite lower headline import values. Once India's EV and solar industries are built around Chinese cell chemistry, Chinese wafer specifications, and Chinese process equipment, switching is not a 6-month logistics problem. It is a 10-year industrial rebuild. The supplier changes from Riyadh to Beijing. The leverage does not reduce. It disappears."

Why oil and mineral dependency are structurally different — not equivalent
Oil is a flow commodity consumed continuously — every barrel imported creates no lock-in for tomorrow. Critical minerals go into capital goods with 8–15 year lifecycles. A battery gigafactory built on Chinese LFP technology in 2026 runs on that technology until 2038+. The dependency is not annual — it is structural and technologically embedded. Oil has partial substitutes (LNG, ethanol, hydrogen) tradeable on liquid global markets. Battery-grade lithium sulphate processed to Chinese specification has no short-term substitute for factories built around that specification. The comparison is valid as a strategic framing — not as an economic equivalence. The correct claim: critical mineral dependency may prove more strategically rigid and harder to exit, not that the headline import values are identical.

03 — Leontief Contagion Mechanics (Operationalised in v3.0)

Industrial Dependency Propagation — From Concept to Mechanics

Prior versions referenced Leontief economics as theoretical grounding. v3.0 operationalises the actual propagation mechanics. The I-O framework reveals that supply disruptions do not stay contained — they propagate through industrial linkages in three waves.

Leontief Propagation Model — Three-Wave Structure
Direct effects (Wave 1): The sector immediately deprived of the input. A nickel export restriction directly hits battery cathode manufacturers and stainless steel producers.

Indirect effects (Wave 2): Sectors that supply or depend on the directly affected sector. EV manufacturers cannot source battery packs → EV assembly lines halt → steel demand for EV chassis drops → logistics sector loses freight volume. Each hop multiplies the disruption by the Leontief multiplier for that sector.

Induced effects (Wave 3): Economy-wide income and consumption effects as affected workers and businesses reduce spending. In India's context, this matters less than the first two waves for near-term supply chain analysis, but becomes significant if disruption persists beyond 6 months.

Chokepoint identification: A node with high betweenness centrality in the I-O graph — meaning many industrial paths pass through it — is a chokepoint. Indonesia's nickel mine output and China's lithium refining capacity are the two highest-centrality nodes in India's electrification supply chain. Restricting either propagates disruption to the maximum number of downstream sectors simultaneously.

Practical cost inflation propagation — worked example
Event: Indonesia announces a 20% cut in nickel mining quotas (as occurred in 2025 for price support).

Wave 1 — Direct (Days 1–30): LME nickel price rises 15–25%. Battery cathode precursor cost (NMC811) increases $800–1,200/tonne. Stainless steel coil prices rise 8–12%.

Wave 2 — Indirect (Days 30–90): Indian battery pack assemblers (Amara Raja, Exide) face input cost inflation of 6–9% on NMC packs. EV manufacturers (Tata Motors, MG Motor) absorb or pass through. Indian stainless steel imports (already 35% of consumption) become more expensive → construction and process industry cost inflation of 2–4%.

Wave 3 — Induced (Months 3–12): If disruption persists, India's solar installation costs rise as module mounting structures use stainless steel. CAPEX for greenfield industrial projects increases 3–6% due to structural steel inflation. Battery storage project financing becomes more expensive as IRR assumptions shift.

Leontief multiplier applied: Using a conservative output multiplier of 2.2 for nickel, a $500M annualised supply disruption in nickel-dependent sectors generates approximately $1.1B in downstream GDP impact across the Indian electrification industrial complex.

05 — Three-Layer Dependency Structure

The Risk Is in Layer 2 and Layer 3

The analytical error most policy analysis makes: stopping at Layer 1 (the mine) and concluding mineral security is managed. The structural risk concentrates at Layer 2 (processing) and Layer 3 (equipment) — neither of which appears in India's standard trade risk monitoring.

Vietnam Re-Labelling — The Hidden Layer
India's solar import data shows China's cell import share falling from 90% to 56% (FY2022–FY2025). This is presented as diversification. Vietnam, Malaysia, and Thailand source 85–95% of their polysilicon and wafers from China. The module is assembled outside China. The upstream supply chain is not. When China imposes polysilicon export controls, Vietnam's module supply to India collapses simultaneously. India's diversification dashboard shows improvement. India's actual upstream resilience does not.

The equipment sourcing paradox — Layer 3 in execution
India's most ambitious execution scenario — building domestic battery gigafactories — requires Chinese manufacturing equipment to do so. Electrode coating machines, formation and ageing equipment, dry room systems, electrolyte filling lines, and cell stacking equipment are 80–90% Chinese-manufactured. This creates a paradox: India's strategy to reduce Chinese dependency on batteries requires deepening Chinese dependency on battery equipment. A Chinese export control on manufacturing equipment — not cells, not minerals, but the machines that make the cells — would terminate India's gigafactory programme before it reaches commercial output. This is the Layer 3 lock-in that no PLI scheme currently addresses.

Why the $53B scenario remains the most strategically relevant
S4 (sodium-ion + aggressive recycling) is technically plausible but requires simultaneous execution of CATL Na-ion commercialisation, India battery collection infrastructure, and domestic refining — none of which are on-track. The base case (S2 at $38B) assumes moderate PLI success and partial J&K activation. S1 ($53B) is the trajectory India is currently on, absent a deliberate policy pivot. The framework does not predict which scenario materialises — it identifies which policy interventions are required to move from S1 toward S2 or S3, and what execution constraints (Section 06) make that transition harder than the policy documents acknowledge.

01 — Central Thesis (Corrected Framing)

The Substitution India Isn't Discussing

⚠
v2.0 Framing Correction — Critical Issue 3
The v1.0 claim "critical minerals are worse than oil" was directionally correct but structurally imprecise. Oil is a flow commodity consumed continuously. Batteries are capital goods with multi-year lifecycles. The economic mechanisms differ. The corrected framing below is more precise and substantially harder to attack.

"Critical mineral dependency may become more strategically rigid and technologically irreversible than oil dependency — despite lower headline import values. Once India's EV and solar industries are built around Chinese cell chemistry, Chinese wafer specifications, and Chinese equipment, switching is not a 6-month logistics problem. It is a 10-year industrial rebuild."

Why the oil comparison must be qualified
Oil is continuously consumed — every barrel imported today creates no lock-in for tomorrow. Critical minerals go into capital goods with 8–15 year lives. A battery gigafactory built on Chinese LFP technology in 2026 runs on that technology until 2038+. The dependency is not annual — it is structural. Furthermore, oil has partial substitutes (LNG, ethanol, hydrogen). Battery-grade lithium sulphate processed to Chinese specification has no short-term substitute for the factories built to that specification. The import dependency operates through technological lock-in, not price volatility — which makes it harder to detect, harder to quantify, and harder to exit.

04 — Three-Layer Dependency Framework

Where the Risk Actually Lives

Most policy analysis stops at Layer 1. The analytical error is not seeing Layer 2 (processing) and Layer 3 (equipment) — where China's structural control is near-total and exit timelines are measured in decades.

Hidden Layer — Vietnam Re-Labelling Trap
India's solar import data shows China's share falling from 90% to 56% of cell imports since FY2022. Policymakers cite this as diversification. Vietnam, Malaysia, and Thailand source 85–95% of their polysilicon and wafers from China. The module is assembled elsewhere. The upstream supply chain is not. When China restricts polysilicon exports, Vietnam's module supply to India collapses simultaneously. India's diversification dashboard shows improvement. India's actual supply chain resilience does not.

06 — Battery Chemistry Transition Risk (New in v2.0)

Nickel Risk Is Conditional — Not Deterministic

Critical Issue 5 from the analytical review: the v1.0 framework was heavily nickel-centric without making the chemistry pathway assumption explicit. This section corrects that.

⚗
The Chemistry Conditionality Statement
Nickel's CRITICAL score (10.0) holds under NMC pathway assumptions. It requires explicit reassessment if India's EV fleet chemistry shifts toward LFP at scale, or if sodium-ion commercialisation accelerates for two-wheelers and grid storage. This conditionality is not a weakness of the framework — it is a feature. It identifies the single most important variable to monitor.

Current India signal
India's SUV/MPV segment reached 80% of EV sales in 2025 (up from 60% in 2024). SUVs and premium cars are the most energy-density-sensitive segment — this is a NMC signal, not an LFP signal. India's two-wheeler EV segment, however, is showing LFP adoption for cost reasons. The framework should be read as: nickel risk is HIGH for the car segment, MEDIUM for the two-wheeler segment, and stable for aerospace regardless of EV chemistry shifts.
07 — Recycling Dynamics (New in v2.0)

The Circular Economy Offset — What the Critics Will Say

Critical Issue 4 identified a major omission: the v1.0 framework models a one-way extraction economy. By 2030–2035, urban mining may materially reduce lithium, nickel, and cobalt import intensity. This section acknowledges the offset — and explains why it does not resolve the near-term structural risk.

Lithium recovery 2025
~5%
Global end-of-life Li-ion battery recycling rate. Economics weak. Collection infrastructure immature.
Nickel recovery 2025
~12%
Higher than lithium due to established stainless steel scrap markets. NMC battery nickel recovery improving.
India battery take-back
Nascent
Battery Waste Management Rules 2022 notified but enforcement infrastructure minimal. No significant urban mining capacity yet.
Why current recycling does not offset the 2030 risk: Even at 2030 projections of 25–30% recovery rates globally, the stock of batteries requiring recycling is only as large as the fleet installed 8–10 years prior. India's EV fleet in 2030 is recycling batteries from a 2020–2022 fleet that barely existed. The recycling offset becomes material only from 2035 onward, when the 2025–2030 installation wave reaches end of life. The near-term (2025–2032) critical mineral dependency is structural and cannot be recycling-adjusted away.

The honest 2035 picture: By 2035, if India's Battery Waste Management Rules achieve real enforcement, urban mining could offset 10–20% of lithium demand and 15–25% of cobalt demand. This is meaningful but does not eliminate the structural import dependency created in 2025–2032. It reduces the steady-state dependency ceiling — it does not resolve the transition-period vulnerability.
What India currently lacks
No commercial black mass processing facility at scale. Battery Waste Management Rules 2022 notified without enforcement infrastructure. No EV battery collection network. No hydrometallurgical lithium recovery plant. Extended Producer Responsibility targets exist on paper — implementation is years behind.
What India needs to build (2026–2030)
Centralised battery collection hubs in top 20 EV cities. Two to three hydrometallurgical black mass processing plants (co-located with Adani/Tata gigafactory clusters). EPR enforcement with financial penalties creating market pull. Lithium recovery specifications aligned with battery-grade reuse standards. Government procurement preference for batteries with certified recycled content.
What recycling realistically offsets by 2030
Cobalt: 10–15% import reduction (cobalt most economically attractive to recover). Nickel: 5–10% reduction if NMC battery fleet grows as projected. Lithium: 2–5% reduction — smallest offset, requires most infrastructure investment. Graphite: 5–8% with cathode/anode black mass processing. Net effect on CIIP score: lithium moves from 8.8 to approximately 8.4 — still CRITICAL. Cobalt moves from 7.8 to approximately 7.2 — still HIGH.
08 — China Dominance ≠ Permanent · CIIP v3.0 Vision

The Multipolar Transition — And Why It Doesn't Solve 2026–2032

Critical Issue 6: v1.0 insufficiently modelled the counter-forces to Chinese dominance. IRA, EU CRM Act, Indonesia refining build-out, Adani Mundra, African processing investments — all are real. The honest answer: the transition to multipolarity takes 8–12 years to materialise. The window of maximum exposure is now.

First Multipolar Signals
US IRA gigafactories coming online. EU CMA-driven diversification starting. Indonesia nickel processing becoming partially independent. Adani polysilicon commissioning. CIIP scores begin moderate reduction.
2030–2033
Structural Diversification
African refining investments mature. Australian lithium-to-chemicals plants scale. India PLI second generation results visible. Sodium-ion commercialisation impact measurable. China share falls from 70–90% toward 50–60%.
2033+
Multipolar Equilibrium
Not deglobalised — multipolar. Three to four processing blocs: China, USA/allies, EU/Africa, India/ASEAN. CIIP scores structurally lower but concentrated around new chokepoints (rare earths, advanced cathode IP, solid-state electrolyte).
